Overview

Chaos Classroom is a team-developed game built across multiple semesters — chaotic by design, and demanding to sound right. As Audio Lead, I owned the game's entire sonic identity, from the first scratch tracks to the final implemented build.

My Role

  • Composed original music and soundtracks across the game's arc
  • Designed and produced sound effects to match moment-to-moment gameplay
  • Implemented all audio in-engine, managing states, triggers, and mix balance
  • Set and maintained the overall audio vision for the team across both semesters

Audio Direction

The game's title says it all, Chaos Classroom needed audio that felt energetic and unhinged without becoming noise. Every sound decision had to serve that balance: music that escalated with the action, effects that were readable under pressure, and a mix that never buried the feedback players needed to make good choices. I developed a consistent sonic language early and used it as a reference point for every asset I added across the project's lifespan.

Design Challenges

The hardest part wasn't the composition or the implementation — it was getting the whole team aligned on what the game should sound like. Audio direction is invisible until it's wrong, so I had to make the reasoning behind each choice explicit: presenting references, framing decisions in terms of player experience, and revisiting the vision as the game itself evolved. Getting creative buy-in from a team across multiple semesters taught me how to advocate for sound as a design tool, not an afterthought.

What I Learned

Building audio from scratch for a long-running team project changed how I think about collaboration. Sound shapes tone in ways that are hard to articulate until you hear the difference — so I learned to show rather than describe, and to treat every playtest as a chance to pressure-test not just the mix, but whether the audio was actually communicating what the game needed it to.
